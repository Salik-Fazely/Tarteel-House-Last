PROGRESS

How Parents Can Track Their Child’s Quran Progress

A child may not learn a new surah every month, but that does not mean nothing is changing.

They may recognise Arabic letters more quickly. They may make fewer repeated mistakes. They may remember an older surah with less help or read aloud with more confidence.

Parents can understand Quran progress more clearly by looking at a few simple areas over time: reading, revision, confidence, consistency and the child’s current learning goal. A short check-in every six to eight weeks is often more useful than judging progress after every lesson.

Reviewed by Forouhar Rahmani, Tarteel House Quran Teacher

Reviewed: July 2026

Progress is more than memorising new surahs

It is easy to measure progress only by counting completed pages or memorised surahs. These things matter, but they are not the full picture.

A child may be improving in several ways:

  • Reading accuracy: familiar letters, sounds and words are becoming easier.
  • Memory and revision: the child remembers earlier lessons with less support.
  • Confidence: they are more willing to read aloud and try again after a mistake.
  • Consistency: they attend lessons regularly and follow a manageable home-practice routine.
  • Independence: they need less help with material they have already studied.

A child may improve in one area before another. For example, pronunciation may become clearer even while memorisation still feels slow.

Start with a simple baseline

Before tracking progress, write down a simple picture of where your child is now.

You do not need a formal test or a complicated scorecard. Ask:

  • What is my child learning at the moment?
  • What is the teacher’s current goal?
  • Which part feels easy?
  • Where does my child need the most help?
  • Is home practice calm and manageable, or does it often become difficult?

Keep the answers short. One or two sentences are enough.

The baseline does not need to be perfect. It only gives you something fair to compare with later.

Use a simple six-to-eight-week check-in

You do not need to record something after every lesson. A short review every six to eight weeks can show changes more clearly.

Simple Quran Progress Check-in

Area What to notice
Current learning goal What is the teacher focusing on now?
Reading Are familiar letters, words or lines becoming easier?
Revision Can your child remember earlier work with less help?
Confidence Are they more willing to read aloud and correct a mistake?
Home practice Is the agreed routine manageable for your family?
Next step What is one realistic goal for the coming weeks?

Try to write what you actually notice rather than giving a general score.

For example:

“She now reads the first three lines with fewer pauses, but still needs help with two repeated sounds.”

This is more useful than writing:

“She is doing well.”

Ask the teacher these four questions

Parents do not need to understand every rule of Arabic pronunciation to take part in their child’s learning. Clear communication with the teacher can help.

During a progress review, ask:

  1. 1. What is my child’s main learning goal right now?
  2. 2. What improvement have you noticed since the last review?
  3. 3. Which mistake or difficulty needs the most attention?
  4. 4. What should we practise at home before the next lesson?

Useful feedback should help you understand both progress and the next step.

A simple answer such as “your child is doing well” may feel encouraging, but it does not tell the family what to continue or what to change.

What to do when progress feels slow

Children do not always progress at the same speed. School, tiredness, confidence, family routines and the difficulty of the current lesson can all affect learning.

When progress feels slow:

  • Do not compare your child with a sibling or another student.
  • Do not treat one difficult week as a sign that the lessons are failing.
  • Ask the teacher whether the current goal is still suitable.
  • Check whether the home-practice routine is realistic.
  • Notice small improvements, not only completed surahs or pages.

Progress is not always a new surah. Sometimes it is one familiar line read with less help than before.

If your child regularly feels worried, frustrated or unwilling to join lessons, speak with the teacher. A change in pace, practice method or teaching approach may be worth considering.

A clear picture is better than constant pressure

You do not need to measure your child after every class.

A clear starting point, a few simple observations and regular communication with the teacher can give you a much better picture of progress.

The goal is not only to move quickly. It is to help the child improve steadily while keeping Quran learning calm, clear and manageable.
