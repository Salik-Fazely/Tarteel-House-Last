MEMORIZATION

How to Help Children Memorize Short Surahs

Learn a simple way to help children memorise short surahs through listening, small sections, regular revision and a manageable 10-minute routine.

Helping a child memorise short surahs—chapters of the Quran—does not require one long practice session.

A brief and regular routine can be easier to manage. The child listens to a small section, repeats it carefully, reviews older verses and receives clear correction when needed.

The aim is not only to finish a surah quickly. The child should remember the words, improve their recitation and continue practising without feeling that every session is an exam.

Reviewed by Sadiah Hamid, Tarteel House Quran Teacher

Reviewed: July 2026

Start with a small and suitable section

A short surah is not always easy for every child.

Before beginning, consider what your child can already read and repeat. A child who is still learning Arabic sounds may need a different starting point from a child who reads independently.

The teacher may suggest beginning with one short verse or part of a verse rather than the whole surah.

Before adding new material, ask:

  • Can my child hear and repeat the words clearly?
  • Is the pronunciation manageable at their current level?
  • Does an older section still need revision?
  • Has the teacher suggested this surah or verse as a suitable next step?

A small amount that the child can practise carefully is more useful than a large amount that becomes confusing.

Listen before asking your child to repeat

Listening gives the child a clear example before they try to recite.

Use a recitation recommended by the teacher or another trusted source. It is usually easier for the child when the same reciter and recording are used during practice.

Choose a short section and play it a few times. Avoid moving quickly between different recordings, speeds and recitation styles.

The child can listen once without repeating. After that, pause the recording and ask them to repeat a short phrase.

When you are unsure about a sound or pronunciation, do not guess. Make a note and ask the teacher during the next lesson.

Break the verse into small parts

A full verse may feel difficult when the child hears it all at once.

Divide it into short parts that can be heard and repeated clearly.

A simple process is:

  1. Listen to one short phrase.
  2. Repeat it with the recording or teacher.
  3. Try it without the recording.
  4. Add the next short phrase.
  5. Join the two parts together.
  6. Recite the full verse when the parts feel more familiar.

There is no single number of repetitions that works for every child. One child may remember a phrase after a few attempts, while another needs more listening and support.

Stop before repetition becomes careless or frustrating.

Use a manageable 10-minute routine

Ten minutes can be a useful starting point for home practice, but it is not a strict rule. Some children may need a shorter session, while others can continue a little longer.

Ten-Minute Memorisation Routine

Approximate time Activity
2 minutesReview an older verse or surah
2 minutesListen to the new short section
3 minutesRepeat it in small parts
2 minutesJoin the parts and try without help
1 minuteEnd positively and agree on the next practice

A short session works better when the child knows what they are practising.

Instead of saying, “Let us practise Quran,” give one clear goal:

“Today, we will review yesterday’s verse and learn the first short part of the next one.”

Review before adding something new

Memorisation needs revision. A child may recite a verse correctly today and find it difficult again after a few days.

Before adding a new section, check whether your child can:

  • remember the earlier words with less help;
  • keep the verses in the correct order;
  • recite after a short break;
  • correct an important mistake after a reminder;
  • connect the new part with what came before.

If the earlier section is still weak, spend another day reviewing it. This is not a failure or a delay. Revision is part of memorisation.

Correct mistakes clearly

Children need correction, but stopping them after every small sound can make practice tiring.

Allow the child to finish a short phrase when possible. Then correct one clear point and give them time to try again.

For example:

“Listen to this word once more. Now try it slowly.”

Avoid giving several pronunciation explanations at the same time.

Important mistakes should not be ignored until they become familiar habits. However, parents should not try to teach a pronunciation they are unsure about. Save the difficult word and ask the teacher for guidance.

The aim is not only to remember the words. The child should also learn to recite them as accurately as their current level allows.

Keep older surahs active

A newly memorised surah can become weaker when it is no longer reviewed.

Keep some older material in the weekly routine, even when the child is learning something new.

Example Weekly Routine

Day Main focus
Day 1Review an older surah and begin a new section
Day 2Repeat the same new section
Day 3Review without adding anything new
Day 4Add the next short section
Day 5Join the sections together
WeekendCalm review of older surahs

This is only an example. The teacher may recommend a different pace depending on the child’s age, level and current goal.

What parents should avoid

Home practice can become difficult when the child feels constant pressure.

Try to avoid:

  • replacing several missed days with one very long session;
  • adding a new verse before reviewing the previous one;
  • comparing the child with siblings or other students;
  • correcting pronunciation when you are not sure;
  • using several different recordings for the same section;
  • treating every practice session like a test;
  • measuring progress only by the number of completed surahs.

Praise specific effort rather than giving only a general comment.

For example:

“You remembered the first verse with less help today.”

This tells the child what improved.

When memorisation feels difficult

Difficulty does not always mean the child needs more repetition.

Consider whether:

  • the selected section is too long;
  • the pronunciation is still unfamiliar;
  • the child is tired or distracted;
  • the practice time is unsuitable;
  • earlier verses need more revision;
  • the child understands how the verse is divided;
  • the teacher needs to adjust the pace or method.

A shorter section, a different practice time or more listening may help. When the same difficulty continues, speak with the teacher before increasing the pressure at home.

Keep the routine small and clear

Short, regular practice can make memorisation easier to manage. The quality of listening, correction and revision matters as much as the amount completed.

Begin with a suitable section, keep older surahs active and ask the teacher for help when pronunciation or progress becomes unclear.
