One-to-One Quran Classes vs Group Classes for Children

Parents choosing Quran lessons may wonder whether their child needs individual attention or would benefit from learning with other children.

One-to-one lessons usually offer more personal attention and flexible pacing. Group lessons can provide social interaction and a shared learning routine.

Neither option is automatically right for every child. The better choice depends on the child’s current level, confidence, learning pace, need for correction and comfort when reading in front of others.

Reviewed by Sadiah Hamid, Tarteel House Quran Teacher

Reviewed: July 2026

The main difference is how attention is shared

In a one-to-one Quran class, the teacher works with one child for the full lesson.

This usually gives the child more time to read, answer questions and repeat difficult words or verses. The teacher can also notice repeated mistakes more easily and adjust the lesson around that child’s needs.

In a group class, the teacher’s attention is shared between several children. Each child may spend less time reading individually, but they can also listen to other students and take part in shared activities.

The pace of a group lesson usually needs to work for the class as a whole. This can be helpful when students are at similar levels, but more difficult when their needs are very different.

One-to-one lessons can adapt more easily

One-to-one lessons may be useful when a child needs a more flexible starting point.

For example, one child may still be learning Arabic letters, while another child of the same age may already read from the Quran but need support with pronunciation or Tajweed—the rules used for correct Quran recitation.

An individual lesson can be adjusted more easily when a child:

  • is completely new to Quran reading;
  • has repeated pronunciation difficulties;
  • learns faster or more slowly than expected for their age;
  • needs explanations in a particular language;
  • has a specific goal, such as reading, Tajweed or memorisation;
  • needs more time to answer or repeat;
  • finds it difficult to stay focused while waiting for others.

This does not mean that every child will progress faster in a one-to-one class. Attendance, revision, motivation and the match between teacher and child still matter.

Group lessons can offer shared motivation

Group classes can also have clear benefits.

Some children enjoy learning alongside others. They may feel encouraged when they see other students trying, making mistakes and improving.

A group class may help a child:

  • feel part of a learning community;
  • hear other children read;
  • practise taking turns;
  • enjoy shared activities;
  • build confidence in speaking in front of others;
  • follow a regular group routine.

Group lessons may also be more affordable for families.

However, the experience depends greatly on the size of the group and how closely the students’ levels match. A small group of children with similar needs may work well. A larger group with very different levels may give each child less reading time and less individual correction.

Think about your child’s confidence and personality

A shy child does not always need the same type of class.

Some children feel more comfortable reading alone with one teacher. They may worry less about making mistakes when other students are not listening.

Other children relax when they see that everyone in the group is learning and making mistakes too.

Parents can think about questions such as:

  • Does my child become nervous when reading in front of others?
  • Does my child enjoy learning with other children?
  • Does my child need extra time before answering?
  • Is my child easily distracted by other students?
  • Does my child lose interest when waiting for a turn?
  • Does social interaction help my child stay motivated?

The answers may be more useful than simply choosing based on the child’s age.

Corrections work differently

Correction is an important part of Quran learning, especially when a child is working on reading and pronunciation.

In a one-to-one lesson, the teacher can usually hear more of the child’s reading. This can make it easier to notice patterns, give immediate feedback and allow the child to repeat until the correction is clearer.

In a group lesson, one child’s correction may also help the other students. However, the teacher may not be able to spend a long time on every individual mistake without slowing the whole class.

The quality of correction matters more than the number of corrections.

A child should understand what needs to change and have enough time to try again. Repeatedly being told that something is wrong, without clear guidance, is not very helpful in either type of class.

Which option may suit your child?

One-to-one lessons may suit your child when:

  • they need close correction;
  • their level is different from most children of the same age;
  • they feel uncomfortable reading in front of others;
  • they need lessons in a particular language;
  • their learning goal requires flexibility;
  • they lose focus while waiting for others;
  • they need more time to read and respond.

A group class may suit your child when:

  • they enjoy learning with other children;
  • they can follow a shared pace;
  • they are comfortable reading in front of a group;
  • social participation helps them stay interested;
  • the group is small and students have similar levels;
  • shared activities motivate them;
  • cost is an important part of the family’s decision.

A trial lesson can help when you are still unsure. Watching how your child responds to the teacher and lesson format may give you more useful information than choosing from a description alone.

Questions to ask before choosing

Before enrolling your child, ask the class provider:

  1. How many children are in the group?
  2. Are the children at similar reading levels?
  3. How much time will my child spend reading during each lesson?
  4. How are individual pronunciation mistakes corrected?
  5. Can the pace or material be adjusted?
  6. How will parents receive progress feedback?
  7. What happens if the teacher or class format is not suitable?
  8. Can my child try a lesson before we decide?

Clear answers can help you understand what the lesson will actually be like.

Choose the format that gives your child the right support

The right format is the one that gives your child enough support, participation and comfort to continue learning consistently.

Some children benefit from the focused attention and flexibility of one-to-one lessons. Others enjoy the shared routine and social experience of a group.

The most useful question is not simply, “Which type is better?”

It is:

“Which type gives my child the best chance to read, understand corrections and stay involved in the lesson?”
